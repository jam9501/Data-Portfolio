This project is for Columbia Journalism School's LEDE program. I wanted to explore what different tie knots presidential candidates wore, and if there was a correlation between successful candidates and their choice of knot. Because data on presidential candidates and tie knot choices did not exist in a traditional format, I created my own dataset based on archival footage and photos of presidential debates, etc. I used AI to categorize knots based on the photos. Theodore Roosevelt was the first presidential candidate to wear a traditional tie, so the data looks at candidates after 1908. I only included candidates post-primaries. I coded the bar graph in Python, then stylized it afterwards in Adobe Illustrator, and created the tie graphics using Adobe Illustrator. I animated the photos using D3 + JavaScript: https://jam9501.github.io/presidential-ties/ties.html

Of course, the content and design will change based on the newsrooms’ needs and preferences, but I believe fostering these skills will lead to more engagement and reporting efficiency.

In Jeffrey L. Griffin & Robert L. Stevenson’s research, “The influence of statistical graphics on newspaper reader recall,” two different experiments suggested graphics helped readers digest material more easily.

“The ability to answer and accuracy were highest for the group with the story plus infographic plus photo,” reported the journal, adding the group with the lowest ability was given the text without graphics or photos.

Visualizations helped readers and viewers more ways to take in and learn content— improving accessibility, thus engagement.

AI and programming tools, like scraping websites and building automated datasets, help reporters complete stories faster and cover more investigative content.

I would be honored to join the team, and develop these skills in the newsroom.
