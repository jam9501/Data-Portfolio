Map of Gas Leak Distribution in New York City

I created this map displaying gas leaks in New York City using QGIS, a geographic information system software. The interactive web version below has a tooltip implemented, where the user can hover over a neighborhood and the labels appear. I analyzed U.S. data, and found New York City has the highest rates of gas leaks in the country. Then, I analyzed the Fire Department of New York City's data, and found the Bronx had some of the highest rates of gas leaks within the city. Additionally, Morrisania had more gas leaks than 68% of all New York City neighborhoods. This could be a larger taxpayer story, with new scholarly research suggesting New Yorkers could be paying more than $77 million annually in gas leak repairs.

My journalistic philosophy

My philosophy for reporting is simple: apply the artistry of storytelling to complex fiscal and political ideas. For a journalistic body to reach homeostasis, reporters must find a way to demystify important issues without patronizing readers or viewers.

Democratizing data— putting information directly in consumer’s hands— is a vital step to restoring balance in the press, and combating the spread of misinformation.

And, fostering data and visualization initiatives in newsrooms lead to more engagement and efficient reporting.

Graphics, photos, and visualizations stimulate active learning and help readers digest material, according to Jeffrey L. Griffin and Robert L. Stevenson’s research .

“The ability to answer and accuracy were highest for the group with the story plus infographic plus photo,” reported the journal, adding the group with the lowest accuracy received the text without graphics or photos.

Visualizations provide readers and viewers more ways to interpret content—improving accessibility, thus engagement. AI and programming tools, like scraping websites and building automated datasets, help reporters complete stories faster and cover more investigative content.

We need to build trust in communities through multi-layered representation— diverse reporters and sources— and digestible data.

Researchers know:

  • Latinos are 28% more likely to see content flagged as biased, conspiracy or pseudoscience.
  • 58% of Black Americans say that news coverage of the Black community is too negative.
  • People with disabilities are represented as sources 1% of the time.
  • Lower-income individuals tend to be less confident in finding credible information

Scholars, academics and journalists alike know the problems plaguing the industry—and democracy— but it’s time to develop straightforward solutions. Let’s talk about it.

My story

Growing up, I imagined my first cries not as a defining moment of birth—but one where I pleaded for my whole body back.

As a young girl who had a stroke in the womb, my limited abilities often dictated my interests. I had adolescent dreams of becoming a surgeon or firefighter, but I always found a needle prodding my back of doubt and incurable incompetence.

But, at five-years-old, I sat at a desk, in a tiny plastic chair I couldn’t fit into now, and wrote, “The dog chased the cat.” It wasn’t the sentence heard around the world, but in my own microcosm, the orbit shifted to a whole new trajectory.

As a child, my instinctual malleability of syntax supplemented the natural physical abilities I lacked. My first story, “We Never Had a Library,” introduced a group of children who wanted to read, but never learned how—and followed their journey of building a library for their town. I felt the act of writing grow out of my body like another limb: an extension of myself.

I ran to my elementary school’s librarian to ask if she could publish it. She likely thought the request was cute and decided to humor me, but I’m grateful she did. When I put the laminated label on my book, and placed it on the correct shelf using the Dewey Decimal System, I dropped the notion of dreams, and found my calling.

It’s funny, looking back on its subtle themes of media accessibility. Though I hadn’t grasped the concept yet, I knew people needed resources to educate themselves. Since then, I’ve witnessed intellect act as a vital function for survival. Everyone needs truth to gain power over their own lives.

At eleven-years-old, my plan evolved. Reality interested me far more than fiction ever could, so I decided to be a journalist.

In early high school, this solidified at The Newseum when I saw, “There are three people who run towards danger and not away: Police, firemen, and reporters.” I read the dreams I wanted as a child, and a calling that encompassed them all.
